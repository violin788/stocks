<div style="font-family:Times New Roman;font-size:10pt;font-variant:normal;text-align:justify;margin:0pt 0pt 0pt 8pt;">Abstract data were released today highlighting the potential of the Company&#8217;s gene therapy for Type 1 diabetes.&#xa0; This data were released in conjunction with the upcoming presentation by the Company&#8217;s research collaborators at the University of Pittsburgh (&#8220;Pitt&#8221;) at the 16th International Conference on Advanced Technologies &amp; Treatments for Diabetes (ATTD 2023) taking place February 22-25, 2023 in Berlin, Germany. The presentation will be delivered on February 25th at 2:25 pm CET by Ranjeet S. Kalsi, DO, a member of the laboratory of George Gittes, MD, from Pitt&#8217;s Division of Pediatric Surgery, Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania.&#xa0; The presentation titled, &#8220;Pancreatic Intraductal Infusion of Adeno-Associated Virus (&#8220;AAV&#8221;) to Treat Non-Human Primates in a Toxin-Induced Diabetes Model,&#8221; will report results from eight non-human primates (&#8220;NHPs&#8221;) with toxin-induced diabetes after streptozocin administration.&#xa0; They received a novel infusion process that used an AAV vector to deliver Pdx1 and MafA genes directly to the pancreas. The statistically significant study results show that post-infusion of the AAV engineered construct, the eight NHPs had decreased insulin requirements (p&lt;0.001); increased c-peptide levels (p&lt;0.05); and improved glucose tolerance compared to baseline (p&lt;0.05) with one demonstrating reestablished normoglycemia.
